## Patterns to Adopt

### 1. Sticky Navy Header with Coral CTA Button
**Seen in**: Vista AI (dark sticky nav), Neurode Labs (glass-morphism transparent → solid on scroll), Stairmed (transparent → dark on scroll)
**Why for skinmap**: The sticky dark nav communicates authority and clinical professionalism. Every scroll sees the "Request a Demo" coral button — the highest-converting placement. Pair with a transparent-to-navy scroll transition for a premium feel.
**Implementation**: Start transparent over the hero (white logo), transitions to full navy at ~80px scroll. CTA button stays coral throughout.

### 2. Full-Viewport Hero with Floating iPhone + Dual CTA Tracks
**Seen in**: SkinCheck (app store CTAs + hero mockup), Neurode Labs (centered product showcase), Owlet (FDA claim + product), Vista AI (centered claim + logo row)
**Why for skinmap**: skinmap IS an iPhone app — the product must be visible immediately. Two CTA tracks ("Request a Demo" for physicians, "Ask Your Doctor" for patients) mirror SkinCheck's dual-audience strategy but with a physician-first hierarchy. Left column copy/CTAs, right column floating phone mockup.
**Implementation**: Navy gradient background (135deg); headline + subheadline + 2 CTAs left; phone mockup right (angled, floating with glow shadow). Below hero: logo social-proof bar.

### 3. Outcome-First Stat Cards in Clinical Evidence Sections
**Seen in**: Sword Health (67% pain-free, 83 NPS, 64% depression reduction), Mainstay Medical (VAS ≥50% reduction), Biobeat (FDA K-numbers), Vista AI (real-world metrics callout)
**Why for skinmap**: Physicians respond to clinical data, not marketing claims. Leading with "94.3% sensitivity" in a large stat card is more credible than any paragraph. Format: large number (coral/navy), label below, brief context line.
**Implementation**: 3-column stat grid (navy bg variant): sensitivity %, images validated, clinical sites. Each with Framer Motion counter animation on scroll-enter.

### 4. Logo Trust Bar (Partner / Institutional)
**Seen in**: Vista AI (Brigham & Women's, Stanford, Sutter, GE), Sword Health (Hilton, Domino's, Philips), Cellens (Tufts, Dana-Farber, NIH, NSF), BioCorteX (Bayer, Google, NVIDIA, AWS)
**Why for skinmap**: Physician adoption follows peer institutions. Logos of even one or two recognizable hospital systems or med schools immediately elevate credibility. Position right below the hero.
**Implementation**: Horizontal scrolling ticker on mobile; 5–7 logo row on desktop. Muted/grayscale logos on cream background. Lead text: "Trusted by leading institutions."

### 5. Alternating Section Backgrounds for Visual Rhythm
**Seen in**: Sword Health (white → cream → white), Mainstay (white → cream → teal), FluidAI (dark → card grid → dark)
**Why for skinmap**: Alternating white and cream backgrounds create natural section breaks without heavy dividers, improving scannability. Navy-background sections (like the clinical evidence stats) create dramatic emphasis moments.
**Implementation**: White → Cream → White → Navy (stats) → White → Cream → Navy (final CTA). Sections naturally delineated without borders.

## Patterns to Avoid

### 1. Full-Page Scroll Snap (fullpage.js / Stairmed pattern)
**Seen in**: Stairmed, partially in BioCorteX
**Why to avoid**: Hijacks natural scroll, frustrates users who want to skim, breaks browser back button behavior, and hurts SEO (sections don't have anchors). Physician visitors have very limited time — they need to scan, not be forced through a guided tour.

### 2. Full Black/Dark-Theme Throughout
**Seen in**: FluidAI (black #000000 background throughout)
**Why to avoid**: While dramatic for a surgical AI tool, an all-dark theme feels cold for dermatology. skinmap's product is about skin health, a warm and human domain. A pure dark theme will also undermine trust for the patient audience. Use navy for accents and hero sections only.

### 3. Consumer Serif Fonts for Main Headings
**Seen in**: Hilo (IvyPresto), Owlet (Source Serif Pro)
**Why to avoid**: Serif headings work well for consumer wellness brands but undermine clinical authority. skinmap's physician audience expects the clean, precise feel of Inter or Manrope. Serifs read as "lifestyle brand" not "clinical diagnostic tool."

## Recommended Design Decisions

### Color Palette
The CLAUDE.md palette is **confirmed and strong**. Minor refinement based on competitive analysis:

| Token | Hex | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| `navy` | `#0F1B35` | Unique in the space — most competitors use purple or teal. Navy signals clinical authority. |
| `coral` | `#E87B5A` | **No direct competitor uses coral.** This is skinmap's signature. Warm skin-tone reference is on-brand for dermatology. |
| `blue` | `#4A89E8` | Matches Sword Health's blue — familiar in health tech, strong for badges/links. |
| `cream` | `#F8F6F3` | Sword Health uses #f7f4f2; Mainstay uses #F3F0E4 — warm cream is the medical AI standard for approachable backgrounds. |

**Key insight**: No competitor uses coral as their primary accent. This is a strong differentiator. Lean into it — coral CTAs, coral stat numbers, coral eyebrow labels.

### Typography
**Confirmed: Inter**. Rationale:
- SkinCheck (closest competitor) uses Manrope — Inter differentiates
- Most competitors use Montserrat, Figtree, or Plus Jakarta Sans
- Inter is the most legible at small sizes (important for clinical data tables)
- Inter's tabular numerals are ideal for displaying clinical statistics

Type scale:
- Display headlines: 56–80px, weight 800, tracking -0.02em
- Section headlines: 36–48px, weight 700, tracking -0.01em
- Body: 16–18px, weight 400, line-height 1.65
- Eyebrow/badge: 11–12px, weight 600, uppercase, tracking 0.1em

### Hero Section
**Left-right split layout** (60/40):
- Left: Eyebrow badge (coral) → bold headline (white, 64px) → subheadline (gray-300, 18px) → two CTA buttons (coral primary + outline secondary) → trust micro-copy ("HIPAA compliant · FDA pathway · IRB-validated")
- Right: Floating iPhone mockup at 15° angle, coral glow shadow underneath, subtle floating animation
- Background: Navy gradient (135deg, #0F1B35 → #1A3060 → #0F1B35)
- Below hero: Full-width cream social proof bar with grayscale partner logos

**Physician CTA**: "Request a Demo" (coral, solid)
**Patient CTA**: "Ask Your Doctor About skinmap" (white outline)

### Navigation Style
**Transparent → Navy sticky** transition:
- Initial state: transparent, white logo + white links + coral "Request a Demo" button
- Scrolled state (>80px): full navy background, same elements
- Desktop: Logo left | Product · About · Investors · News · Contact center | "Request a Demo" right
- Mobile: Hamburger → full-width slide-down navy panel with stacked links + both CTAs

### Trust Signal Approach
Layer three tiers of trust, in increasing specificity:
1. **Institutional logos** (hospital partners, university affiliations) — just below hero
2. **Outcome metrics** in large stat cards — clinical evidence section (sensitivity %, images validated, study count)
3. **Regulatory/compliance badges** inline near relevant content — HIPAA badge on data privacy mentions, FDA pathway callout in hero micro-copy, IRB notation near study citations

The **Biobeat pattern** of listing actual FDA clearance K-numbers is aspirational once cleared. Until then, "On the FDA De Novo Pathway" is a credible placeholder.

### Mobile App Presentation
Three techniques to combine, informed by Neurode Labs, SkinCheck, and Owlet:
1. **Hero mockup**: Single phone at 15° angle, real app UI visible on screen, floating with drop shadow
2. **Feature deep-dive**: Side-by-side phone + feature description (alternating left/right per feature)
3. **Workflow section**: Three phones in a horizontal progression showing the 3-step flow (Capture → Analyze → Track)

## Competitive Intelligence: skincheck.health

skinmap's closest direct analog is SkinCheck. Key differentiators to emphasize throughout the site:

| Dimension | SkinCheck | skinmap (positioning) |
|---|---|---|
| Audience | Consumer/patient first | **Physician first** — clinical tool |
| Distribution | App Store direct | **Physician-mediated** — enterprise B2B |
| Workflow | Patient self-scan | **Physician-captured** — clinical grade |
| Tracking | Spot monitoring | **Longitudinal** — visit-over-visit comparison |
| Compliance | Implied | **HIPAA explicit**, FDA pathway stated |
| Validation | 200k downloads (social) | **Clinical study data** (scientific) |

**Messaging implication**: skinmap should never call itself a "consumer app." It is a "clinical tool," a "physician's instrument," a "diagnostic co-pilot." The word "clinical-grade" should appear frequently.
